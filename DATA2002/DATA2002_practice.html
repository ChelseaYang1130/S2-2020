# binom.test(freq,p=0.5,alternative="greater")
t0 =  length(diff[diff>0])

Hypothesis: \(H_0:\mu=96\) vs \(H_1:\mu>96, \mu<96,\mu \neq 96\)

Assumptions: Observations are indeendently sampled from a symmetric distribution.

Test statistic: \[T=number~of (D_i>0)\] where \(D_i=X_i-96\). Under \(H_0\), the test statistic follows a binomial distribution with identity \(B(n,\frac{1}{2})\) where n is the number of non-zero differences.

Observed test statistic: \(t_0=number~of(d_i>0)=12\)

P-value:

  • \(H_1:\mu<\mu_0\) - \(P(T \leq 12)=0.0065\)
  • \(H_1:\mu>\mu_0\) - \(P(T \geq 12)=0.0065\)
  • \(H_1:\mu \neq \mu_0\) & \(t_0<\frac{n}{2}\) - \(2P(T \leq 12)=0.0065\)
  • \(H_1:\mu \neq \mu_0\) & \(t_0>\frac{n}{2}\) - \(2P(T \geq 12)=0.0065\)

Conclusion: The p-value is greater than 0.05, we does not reject the null hypothesis. The mean is not equal to / greater than / less than 96.

pval = binom.test(t0,n,p=0.5,alternative="greater")$p.value

Test statistic: Let \(T\) be the number of positive differenceso out of the 11 non-zero differences. Under \(H_0\), the test statistic follows a binomial distribution with the identity \(B(11,\frac{1}{2})\).

Observed test statistic: We observed \(t_0=9\) positive differences in the sample.

P-value: probability of getting a test statistic as or more extreme than what we observed, \(P(T \geq 9)=1-P(T \leq 8)=1-pbinom(8,size=11,prob=\frac{1}{2})\approx 0.0327\)

Conclusion:

  • As p-value is smaller than 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis. The population mean of two samples are the same.

  • As p-value is smaller than 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis. The population mean of two samples are different.
